I. The Cosmic Order

The inscription begins with the creation of the universe: "When the blue sky was created above and the dark earth below..."

This is the foundation of Turkic legitimacy. The Kagan was not just a warlord; he was the center of a cosmic link. His rule was mandated by Tengri (Heaven). If the Kagan ruled wisely, the seasons would be regular and the herds would multiply. Political stability mirrored cosmic harmony.

II. The Architect of the Empire

The text describes the Kagan as one who "organized and created" (Il etmis). Leadership was more than power; it was about building institutions.

III. The Sword and The Shield


"I did not sleep by night nor rest by day. I shed my red blood and sweated my black sweat for the people."

Warfare was the circulatory system of the empire. The "Iron Gate" in the west and the "Forest of Qadirkhan" in the east were not just borders‚Äîthey were proofs of divine favor.

IV. The Eternal Stone

Preserving Memory

Why write on stone? Paper burns, silk rots, but stone endures. These steles were a political testament meant to defeat time itself. They were placed at crossroads so all could read them.

Unified Identity

The inscriptions created a shared consciousness. By reading "We, the Turks," a dispersed nomad felt part of a single, great entity. It was nation-building through literacy.

The Warning

The most chilling part of the text is the warning: "Do not be deceived by soft silk and sweet words." It teaches that assimilation and loss of culture are more dangerous than any army.

VI. A Modern Lens: Harari's Interpretation

In his book Sapiens, historian Yuval Noah Harari discusses the unique human ability to cooperate in large numbers through "Imagined Orders".

"You can never convince a monkey to give you a banana by promising him limitless bananas after death in monkey heaven... But you can convince millions of people to build a cathedral or go to war."

The Orkhon Inscriptions are a prime example of creating an "Imagined Order." The "Eternal El" (State) and the concept of a unified "Turkic People" did not exist physically in nature. They were stories‚Äîmyths‚Äîcarved into stone by the Kagans.

By establishing this shared myth, the Kagans allowed thousands of strangers from different tribes to trust one another, pay taxes, and fight for a single cause. The stone was the "mass media" of its time, cementing the fiction that made the empire real.
